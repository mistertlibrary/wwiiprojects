Building Your Lesson

Building Your Lesson

You located sources, but now you have to teach them. Here is how to put the pieces together.

Before your group can teach the class, each person needs to find and annotate five sources: two database articles, one written primary source, and two visual primary sources. Here is what counts for each.

  • 1
    Database Article or Secondary Source

    Use one of the databases listed above. Print it, read it, and annotate it: underline or highlight key facts, circle names and dates, write reactions and questions in the margin, and so on.

  • 2
    Database Article or Secondary Source

    Find a second article that adds something the first one does not. If the first gives the big picture, the second might focus on a specific person or event. Annotate it the same way.

  • 3
    Written Primary Source

    Something created during the war itself: a newspaper article, government document, letter, or diary entry. The person who wrote likely did not know how the war would end. That is what makes it different from everything else in your portfolio.

    Start with the databases. You can also try Chronicling America (newspapers.loc.gov), the National Archives (archives.gov), the FDR Presidential Library (fdrlibrary.org), or the U.S. Holocaust Memorial Museum (ushmm.org).
  • 4
    Visual Primary Source

    A photograph, map, political cartoon, or poster from the period. Find it through a database or authoritative source so you can verify where it comes from and who made it.

    Try: Library of Congress Prints and Photographs (loc.gov/photos), National Archives catalog (catalog.archives.gov), or the National WWII Museum Digital Collections (nationalww2museum.org).
  • 5
    Visual Primary Source

    A second visual that shows something different from your first. Two photographs of the same thing are not two sources! Two sources reveal two different angles on your topic.

Annotating is not optional. We have no trouble believing you printed out an article. Annotations prove you actually read it. Aim for at least three marks per source: something that surprised you, something you do not fully understand, and something you want to bring up in your lesson.

Your group is teaching a mini-lesson on a shared theme. The lesson has three parts, and everyone has a role. Here is how a well-run lesson is structured.

1
Group Introduction (3–5 bullet points total)

Before anyone presents their individual topic, the whole group briefly frames the theme. What is the big picture? Why does this theme matter in the context of World War II? Keep it focused: three to five points, no more. Think of it as setting the stage before each person walks on.

2
Your Topic (3–4 bullet points)

Each person presents their individual topic. Do not read your notes word for word. Tell the class what your topic is, why it mattered during the war, and what surprised you about it. Three to four focused points; stay on track.

3
Your Source (1 source, 2–3 questions)

Show the class one source from your portfolio. Introduce it, explain what it is and where it comes from, and ask two or three questions that make the class actually think about what they are seeing. See Tab 3 for how to do this well.

Group rule: No more than two of the same source type across the whole group. If two people are already presenting photographs, the third person needs to bring something else: a map, a cartoon, a video clip, a document.
Communicate early. Decide who is using what type of source before presentations start, not the morning they are due.

Showing a source is not the same as presenting it. If you put a photograph on the screen and say "here is my source," that teaches nothing. You have to open it up for the class. Before you ask any questions, answer three of your own.

1
What is it?

Name the source type and where it comes from. "This is a photograph taken by the Army Signal Corps in 1944" or "This is an editorial cartoon published in the Chicago Defender during the war." The class needs to know what kind of thing they are looking at before they can think about it.

2
Who made it, and for whom?

Every source was made by someone with a purpose. A government poster is not the same thing as a private letter. A newspaper aimed at Black Americans is not the same as one aimed at white Americans. The creator and the intended audience shape what the source says and what it leaves out.

3
What does it tell us that a textbook cannot?

What does this source reveal about how people lived, felt, or thought during the war? What can you see or read in it that you cannot find in a general summary? That gap between the textbook and the source is where the real history is.

Presenting without opening

"This is a poster from World War II encouraging women to work in factories." [moves on]

Opening the source

"This is a 1943 government poster aimed at women on the home front. Notice what it offers her: not just a job, but pride. The government was not just asking for her labor. It was asking her to change how she thought about herself."

After you present your source, you ask the class two or three questions. The goal is not to quiz people on facts. The goal is to get them thinking about what the source means. That requires a different kind of question.

A weak question has one right answer that anyone could guess without looking at the source. A strong question requires the class to actually engage with what you just showed them.

  • Too thin What year was this photograph taken?
  • Too thin What does the poster say?
  • Makes them think Based on this photograph, what can you tell about conditions for soldiers that an official military report probably would not mention?
  • Makes them think This cartoon appeared in a Black newspaper during the war. What does it suggest about how Black Americans felt about their role in the war effort?
  • Makes them think This was produced by the U.S. government. Does knowing that change how you read it? Why or why not?
Write your questions before presentation day, not during it. The best questions come from spending real time with the source and noticing something that surprises you or that you cannot quite explain.
